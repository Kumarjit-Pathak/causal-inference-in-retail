Causal Inference for Retail: A Survival Guide

Phase 1: The Correlation Trap and Data Shenanigans

Welcome, future data scientists. Today, we are going to talk about a concept that ruins more business decisions than bad coffee in the breakroom: mistakenly trusting correlation. You have probably heard the phrase "correlation does not imply causation." You might have even nodded sagely when your statistics professor said it. But when you are staring at a massive dataset predicting retail sales, it is shockingly easy to forget.

The Scene of the Crime

Imagine you are working as an analyst for a major retail chain across four Indian cities. You have data for 100 different packaged food products over three years. Your boss wants to know: Are our discounts actually working?

You boot up your favorite environment, load in the data consisting of 62,400 rows, and run a quick Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA). You check the correlation matrix and run a naive regression predicting sales volume based on discount depth.

Naive Estimate Result:
For every 1% increase in discount depth, sales volume increases by +520 units per week.

Your boss rejoices. "Great work! Let us aggressively drop prices everywhere!"

Stop right there. You might have just cost the company millions. This is the correlation trap in action.

Enter the Confounder: Brand Equity

Why is that +520 unit number a lie? Through our EDA, if we look closely at the data distributions, we spot something suspicious. High-equity brands (the famous name brands everyone loves) are highly correlated with BOTH discount depth and sales volume.

In retail, popular brands often have the negotiating power to run deeper promotional discounts. Because they are popular, they also sell more units by default. The naive regression model does not know this. It looks at the data and thinks the discount is doing all the heavy lifting, when in reality, the brand's reputation is doing half the work. The brand reputation is a confounder—a pesky invisible string pulling both the treatment (the discount) and the outcome (the sales), thereby creating a false illusion of brilliance.

Simpson's Paradox: When the Data Flips Upside Down

It gets weirder. Have you ever seen a trend that looks completely true overall, but when you break the data down into groups, the trend completely reverses? This mind-bending phenomenon is called Simpson's Paradox.

Let us look at a simplified example of how this destroys naive marketing analysis. Imagine we are looking at two cities: Mumbai (a massive market with high base sales) and Hyderabad (an emerging market with lower base sales).

Simpson's Paradox in Action: The Disappearing Discount

Imagine Mumbai does not use discounts much, but sells a ton of units simply because it is Mumbai. Hyderabad uses massive discounts, but since it is a smaller market, it still sells fewer units than Mumbai.

If you just shove all the data into one big spreadsheet without adjusting for the City (another confounder!), the naive model thinks that increasing discounts lowers sales. But within each city, discounts actually raise sales!

If we use proper causal inference to adjust for these confounders (brand equity, city size, festival seasonality), the true effect of the discounts drops from the naive +520 units to +468 units. That gap is pure, unadulterated bias.

Phase 2: Drawing Causal Spaghetti (DAGs)

We established that our naive model is utterly confused by confounders. So how do we fix it? We draw a map. A very specific, slightly terrifying map called a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG). It is basically causal spaghetti, but it tells us exactly what we need to control for.

The Causal Story

A DAG forces us to write down all our assumptions about what causes what. In our retail pricing scenario, our DAG encodes a clear story:

The Anatomy of A Retail Promotion

Every variable plays a role. We must identify our targets (treatments), what we want to change (outcomes), and the villains messing up our math (confounders).

Role Variables Meaning
The Villains (Confounders) brand_equity, seasonality, is_festival, competitor_price, city_id Cause BOTH the treatment and outcome. We MUST adjust for these.
The Levers (Treatments) discount_depth, instore_display, sms_blast, 2x_points The marketing actions we actually control.
The Goal (Outcomes) sales_volume, revenue, profit_margin What we want to improve.

Building the DAG in Code

Enough talking. Let us write some Python. We use the networkx library to formalize these relationships. Here is the actual code from our workflow:

import networkx as nx

# Initialize the graph
dag = nx.DiGraph()

# Define the confounding paths (Villains → Levers)
confounder_to_treatment = [
    ("brand_equity", "discount_depth"),
    ("brand_equity", "instore_display"),
    ("is_festival", "discount_depth"),
    ("is_festival", "instore_display"),
    ("city_id", "sms_blast")
    # ... and many more
]

# Define direct effects (Villains → Goal)
confounder_to_outcome = [
    ("brand_equity", "sales_volume"),
    ("is_festival", "sales_volume"),
    ("competitor_price", "sales_volume")
]

# Define what we actually care about (Levers → Goal)
treatment_to_outcome = [
    ("discount_depth", "sales_volume"),
    ("instore_display", "sales_volume"),
    ("sms_blast", "sales_volume")
]

dag.add_edges_from(confounder_to_treatment)
dag.add_edges_from(confounder_to_outcome)
dag.add_edges_from(treatment_to_outcome)

print(f"DAG has {dag.number_of_nodes()} nodes and {dag.number_of_edges()} edges")
print(f"Is DAG (acyclic): {nx.is_directed_acyclic_graph(dag)}")

The Backdoor Criterion (Pearl, 2009)

In causal inference, a "backdoor path" is a sneaky, non-causal path that connects our treatment to our outcome. It leaks bias into our estimates. According to causal inference legend Judea Pearl, if we want to know the true, isolated effect of a discount on sales, we must "close the backdoor paths."

The House Analogy

Imagine estimating the effect of a Treatment on an Outcome is like trying to measure the breeze coming through the Front Door of a house.

If the Back Door is also open, a draft blows all the way through the house. You can no longer tell how much breeze is actually coming from the Front Door versus the Back Door. To measure the Front Door's true breeze, you must slam the Back Door shut.

In our case, the path discount_depth ← brand_equity → sales_volume is a backdoor path. It exists because high brand equity naturally leads to both higher sales AND deeper discounts. If we run a regression without addressing it, the effect of brand equity "blows through the back door" and inflates our discount estimate.

How do we close it? By mathematically conditioning on it. In a regression, this simply means including the confounder as a variable in the model (e.g., adding BrandEquity directly alongside Discount when calculating the effect). By holding the confounder constant in the math, the backdoor slams shut. We conditioned on the set {brand_equity, seasonality, is_festival, competitor_price, city_id}, effectively blocking every known backdoor and leaving only the pure causal signal.

Phase 3: The Double Machine Learning Magic (Causal Estimation)

Now that our DAG map is drawn, we need to do the actual math. Our goal is to calculate the Average Treatment Effect (ATE). In basic terms: if we increase the discount depth, how much does sales volume increase on average across the entire population, after stripping out the bias?

Stripping the Bias with DoWhy

We use the fantastic DoWhy library to apply the Backdoor Criterion mathematically via Linear Regression. Here is the exact code we ran to estimate the ATE against our naive model:

Averages are Dangerous: Moving to CATE

The ATE is great for a high-level summary, but marketers do not want averages. "On average" is how people drown in rivers that are three feet deep on average. We need to know where these levers work best. Do SMS blasts work better in Mumbai? Do discounts work better on premium brands?

This is where we hunt for the Conditional Average Treatment Effect (CATE). To find it, we deploy a technique called Double Machine Learning (DML).

How Double Machine Learning (DML) Works

DML relies on a brilliant two-stage "residualization" process (Chernozhukov et al., 2018). It sounds like sorcery, but it essentially isolates the pure causal signal.

Stage 1: The Nuisance Models

We use flexible ML tracking to predict outcomes and treatments directly from our confounders. This captures the bias.

Stage 2: The Causal Model

We subtract the predictions from the reality to get "residuals." We regress these residuals against each other to find the true, pure causal impact.

DML in Action (EconML)

We use Microsoft's EconML library to fit a LinearDML estimator. We pass in our Treatments (T), Outcomes (Y), Confounders (W), and finally, our Effect Modifiers (X)—the variables we want to split by, like City ID and Brand Equity.

from econml.dml import LinearDML
from sklearn.ensemble import GradientBoostingRegressor
from sklearn.multioutput import MultiOutputRegressor

# Initialize LinearDML with Gradient Boosting nuisance models
linear_dml = LinearDML(
    model_y=GradientBoostingRegressor(n_estimators=100, max_depth=4),
    model_t=MultiOutputRegressor(GradientBoostingRegressor(n_estimators=100, max_depth=4)),
    cv=3, random_state=42
)

# Fit the two-stage model
# Y = sales, T = treatments, X = effect modifiers (City, Brand), W = confounders
linear_dml.fit(Y=Y, T=T, X=X, W=W)

# Extract CATE heterogeneity coefficients
print(linear_dml.coef_)

Because footfall is naturally high—shoppers are already buying! A naive correlation matrix would tell you discounts during Diwali are genius, but DML proves you are just throwing margin away.

Phase 4: Stress Testing & Strategic Dominance

Why Do We Use Refutation?

Here is the terrifying secret of Causal Inference: You cannot observe the counterfactual.

In traditional Machine Learning, you predict a label, and then you check your test dataset to see if you were right. You have a "ground truth." In Causal inference, if you give a customer a discount, you can never know what that exact same customer would have done at that exact same time if they had NOT received the discount. There is no test set. There is no ground truth!

Because we cannot verify our answers directly against a test set, we have to try to break our model. If our model is structurally flawed (i.e. we messed up our DAG and left a backdoor open), it will usually fail a Refutation Test.

  • Placebo Treatment: We replace our real discount data with complete random noise. We ask the model, "What is the causal effect of this random noise?" If the model says the noise increases sales, our model is hallucinating. The effect must mathematically drop to zero.
  • Random Common Cause: We conjure a fake, random variable and add it to our dataset as a confounder. If our model identified the true causal map, adding a completely irrelevant variable should not budge the final estimate at all.
  • Data Subsets: We estimate the effect on a random 70% subset of the data. If the estimated causal effect changes drastically, it implies our model is just memorizing noise and is not robust.

Here is the data-driven verdict:
